Income Spent on Housing in Rockford

 

Housing is the single biggest expense for the vast majority of Americans.

The role housing plays in overall health is obvious when one considers the physical harm that can come from not having a dry, temperature controlled, safe place to stay.

Less obvious is the role housing has on mental wellbeing. When it is harder to find or afford housing, it is harder to leave an abuser, leave a problematic home life, or many other situations. Threat of homelessness and the financial burden of housing weigh heavy on mental wellbeing.

The graph to the left shows the distribution each income bracket spends on housing as a percentage of their income. Notable are the fact that almost all of the poorest households, those in the 0-$20k annual income range, spend over 50% of their income on housing. Considering that Housing Authorities are prohibited from charging more than 30% of a resident's income for housing and are meant to provide housing for these low-income individuals, it appears there is a severe lack of public housing available.

Additionally, the poorest residents often obtain housing by renting off landlords. This means the money they spend on housing does not go toward making them wealthier the same way a homeowner with a mortgage does. This graph does not capture the amount of renters vs homeowners, but other graphs on the page about Community Context and the page about Built Environment do.

Where is everyone relative to the Poverty Line?

 

This graph divides the population of Winnebago County by where their income falls relative to the poverty line.

Between 2015 and 2019 there was substantial growth in those earning between 0 and 50% of the poverty line as well as those earning between 50% and 200%.

Over the same time period, there was a growth in those earning more than 500% of the poverty line. This matches a trend across the United States in which the middle class shrinks while the poorest and riches grow.

Noteworthy is that data for 2020, the first year drastically affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, are not yet available. Because there was an existing trend in a growing poor population, is it likely that this was exaccerbated as many lost their jobs due to the pandemic.

Where is everyone relative to the Poverty Line?

 

This chart shows the same information as the one above but divides the population into narrower brackets and displays all of the years between 2015 and 2019.

Year to year, there is considerable variation in most of the brackets with much of the variation being in those earning less than 150% of the poverty line.
